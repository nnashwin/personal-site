So You want to Learn Another Language?

2021-08-28

I heard you want to learn another language.

Maybe you want to visit a foreign country. Maybe you want to move to a foreign country.

Maybe you want to meet new friends. Maybe you are looking for a new direction in life.

These are all sufficent reasons to learn another language.

But ask yourself, do you really want to learn?

Do you want to go through the same struggle as when you were young? Do you want to understand nothing, only able to gather half-correct context clues from a native speaker's body language?

Do you want to look down at a menu and not knowing what to eat?

Do you want to look foolish as you walk into the wrong restroom or say something rude?

Do you want to spend late nights puzzling through some text or television show you can barely understand?

Learning a foreign language is like learning most new things. You must enter a new, uncomfortable environment in order to grow.

The environment may feel fun and fresh. At times it will certainly feel uncomfortable.

Does any of that sound unpleasant to you? Are you still with me?

If so, then I will ask you more.

How will you study? How will you learn?

Classes will not teach you. Teachers can not do learn for you.

Oh, they can give you an environment. They can tell you stories and give lectures about grammatical structures that may only apply half the time.

The best teachers may provide you with native materials and explanations as to why things are happening. They will speak to you in their native language, your adopted language.

They can push you to do work on the outside and try to motivate you to immerse yourself.

Even the best teachers can't do the work for you. Even the best classes can't learn for you.

As they say in software, a computer science degree will not teach you to program. Or its converse: You can learn to program without a computer science degree.

For the actual skills, and the spoils, you will need to walk this path on your own.

Your Syllabus

Here is what will actually work.

Listening to native audio.

Reading native material.

Watching bad reality television in your adopted language.

Watching what you think is great television in your adopted language.

Watching cartoons in your adopted language with only native subtitles.

Reading comic books in your adopted language.

Developing interests in your adopted language.

Looking up words in the dictionary.

Not looking up words in the dictionary and trying to guess their meanings by context clues.

Struggling to remember a word and saying the wrong thing. Feeling surprised as you effortlessly use a new word you heard 2 weeks ago and didn't realize you remembered.

All of these things will build your proficiency when immersed in a large vat of time--an enormous vat, full of hours of ignorance and struggle.

But then, suddenly, you will stumble upon things you understand.

You will get a joke said on a tv show

You will remember a reference to a story you read.

You will be able to share a cultural reference to your group of friends who don't speak the language.

You might even recognize your favorite food item on a foreign language menu.

These small, incremental wins will build until you understand the foreign language most of the time.

As you work on your pronunciation and speaking, you will spit out words in the correct pronunciation with the correct meanings.

You will be understood.

After another, smaller vat of time, you will be able to live in the language, attaining a level close to a native speaker.

Then you will need to make a choice:

Do I keep going on this track?

Or do I move on to something else?
